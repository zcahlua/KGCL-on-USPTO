                                                         Abstract
 1             Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) often fail to capture the link-specific structural
 2             patterns essential for accurate link prediction, since their node-centric message
 3             passing might overlook the subgraph structures connecting two nodes. Prior
 4             attempts to inject such structural context either suffer from high computational cost
 5             or rely on oversimplified heuristics (e.g., common neighbor counts) that cannot
 6             capture multi-hop dependencies. We propose SP4LP (Shortest Path for Link
 7             Prediction), a new framework that integrates GNN-based node encodings with
 8             sequence modeling over shortest paths. Specifically, SP4LP first computes node
 9             representations with a GNN, then extracts the shortest path between each candidate
10             node pair and processes the sequence of node embeddings with a sequence model.
11             This design allows SP4LP to efficiently capture expressive multi-hop relational
12             patterns. Theoretically, we show that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than both
13             standard message-passing GNNs and several leading structural feature methods,
14             positioning it as a general and principled framework for link prediction in graphs.
15             Empirically, SP4LP sets a new state of the art on many standard link prediction
16             benchmarks.


17   1    Introduction
18   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are widely adopted for link-level tasks such as link prediction [24, 44,
19   47], link classification [7, 28, 37] and link regression [11, 22] with applications spanning recommender
20   systems [42], knowledge graph completion [27], and biological interaction prediction [17].
21   Despite their popularity, standard GNNs struggle to accurately represent links, as they typically
22   construct link embeddings by aggregating the representations of the two endpoint nodes. This
23   node-centric strategy leads to a key limitation: structurally distinct links may be mapped to the
24   same representation when their endpoints are automorphic [4, 31, 45]. For example, in the graph of
25   Figure 1, links (v, u) and (v, u′ ) yield identical representations under any standard GNN, even if one
26   pair shares a common neighbor and the other does not. This issue, known as the automorphic node
27   problem [4], highlights a fundamental expressivity bottleneck in message-passing schemes for link
28   representation.
29   To address this, several methods enhance GNNs with structural features (SFs), which can be broadly
30   classified into three paradigms [38]: SF-then-GNN, which injects structural context into the graph
31   before message passing (e.g., SEAL [45], NBFNet [48]); SF-and-GNN, which computes SFs and
32   node embeddings in parallel (e.g., Neo-GNN [43], BUDDY [4], MPLP [12]); and GNN-then-SF,
33   which applies message passing once to compute node representations and then combines them using
34   task-specific structural context (e.g., NCN and NCNC [38]).
35   While SF-then-GNN methods are expressive, they are computationally inefficient, often requiring
36   subgraph extraction or retraining per link. SF-and-GNN models are efficient but rely on predefined

38   compelling trade-off between expressivity and scalability, but current methods in this class, i.e., NCN
39   and NCNC, still rely on overlap between the neighborhoods of the endpoints: NCN uses common

46   connecting the two endpoints. These node embeddings, obtained
47   via a base GNN, are then processed as a sequence using a dedicated
48   sequence model, such as a Transformer [33], LSTM [15], or an
49   injective summation function [39].

59   to distinguish non-automorphic links even when their endpoints are automorphic, thereby overcoming
60   the automorphic node problem. We formally prove that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than most

62   SP4LP is efficient and scalable. The message-passing step is performed only once on the entire
63   graph, and the shortest path computation is a preprocessing step. Unlike SF-then-GNN methods such
64   as SEAL or NBFNet, SP4LP avoids costly per-link subgraph extraction or online traversal during
65   inference, allowing it to scale to large graphs and high-throughput settings.
66   Moreover, SP4LP is a general and flexible framework. It can be instantiated with any GNN
67   architecture to compute node embeddings (e.g., GCN [18], GAT [35], GraphSAGE [14]), and
68   supports a range of sequence models for encoding the path structure, from lightweight aggregators to
69   fully expressive recurrent or attention-based models.
70   Finally, SP4LP achieves state-of-the-art performance across several benchmark datasets. Under
71   the challenging HeaRT evaluation protocol [21], it consistently outperforms existing link prediction
72   methods while maintaining competitive inference speed and low memory usage.
73   These properties make SP4LP a principled and practical solution for learning expressive link
74   representations in real-world graph learning scenarios.

           143   3    Related Work
           144   GNNs have been extensively used for link representation tasks. In standard approaches like Graph
           145   Autoencoders (GAE) [19], node embeddings are computed via message passing, and a simple decoder
           146   (e.g., inner product followed by a sigmoid) predicts link existence. While efficient, these models
           147   exhibit limited expressiveness [4, 45], primarily due to their inability to capture rich structural patterns
           148   beyond immediate neighborhoods. This limitation has motivated the integration of explicit structural
           149   information into GNN-based models.
           150   Incorporating Structural Information into GNNs. Several methods inject structural information
           151   into GNN-based link predictors to overcome expressiveness bottlenecks. Neo-GNN [43] adds
           152   handcrafted structural signals to aggregation, while ELPH [4] and its scalable variant BUDDY encode
           153   multi-hop neighborhood patterns via MinHash and HyperLogLog sketches, precomputed offline in
           154   BUDDY for efficiency. MPLP [12] replaces sketching with message passing over quasi-orthogonal
           155   random vectors, yielding unbiased estimates of common neighbor counts and Distance Encoding
           156   labels concatenated with GNN embeddings. NCN [38] aggregates embeddings from endpoints and
           157   their common neighbors, and NCNC extends it by first predicting missing neighbors. NBFNet [48]
           158   instead aggregates over all node-pair paths via Bellman–Ford-inspired recursive updates. Beyond
           159   GNNs, PROXI [32] combines handcrafted proximity indices with XGBoost; while related through
           160   its use of structural signals, it fundamentally differs from GNN-based link representation models.
           161   Despite these advances, such mechanisms still struggle to distinguish more complex structures, as
           162   shown in the next section.
           163   Enhancing GNN Expressiveness through Positional and Structural Encoding. Positional en-
           164   codings further enrich GNN expressiveness. PEG [36] integrates Laplacian-based encodings into

           166   subgraphs and labels nodes via the DRNL scheme before applying a GNN. While expressive, these
           167   methods struggle to scale due to subgraph extraction overhead. In contrast, our model achieves
           168   expressiveness by operating directly on compact, informative shortest-path sequences, enabling better

           170   Shortest-Path and Path-Based Methods in Graph Learning. Path-based representations have
           171   a long history in graph learning, from shortest-path kernels [2] to recent neural approaches that
           172   aggregate information over walks or simple paths for graph- and node-level tasks [5, 6, 25, 41], and
           173   to generative graph transformers built on Eulerian path linearizations [46]. Within link prediction,
           174   Geodesic GNN (GDGNN) [20] aggregates node representations along geodesic connections after
           175   global message passing, typically using permutation-invariant aggregation that abstracts away order-
           176   ing. In contrast, SP4LP is pair-centric and explicitly models the shortest path between two endpoints
           177   as a learnable ordered sequence, yielding link representations that capture directional and positional
           178   structure. Recent results [1] further show that walk-based encodings can fail to distinguish node pairs
           179   that path-based representations separate.

           180   4    SP4LP: An Expressive GNN-then-SF Model for link Representation
           181   Existing GNN link representation models that leverage Structural Features (SF) fall into three
           182   categories [38]: SF-and-GNN, which compute SF and GNN embeddings separately and then
           183   combine them (e.g., Neo-GNN [43], BUDDY [4]); SF-then-GNN, which augment the graph with SF
           184   before applying GNN (e.g., SEAL [44], NBFNet [48]); and GNN-then-SF, which compute GNN
           185   embeddings first and then aggregate them using SF (e.g., NCN, NCNC [38]).
           186   In this work, we adopt the GNN-then-SF paradigm, which combines the scalability advantages
           187   of applying message passing only once, as in the SF-and-GNN setting, with the expressiveness
           188   typical of SF-then-GNN approaches, due to the ability to aggregate over task-specific sets of node
           189   representations. To date, the only existing models that follow this paradigm are NCN and NCNC.
           190   For a more in-depth discussion on the differences between our method, SP4LP and NCN, refer to
           191   Section 4.1. In the following, we introduce the necessary definitions, formally describe the model,
           192   and present theoretical results characterizing its expressive power.

           215   For a graph with n nodes and m edges, single-source BFS [9] runs in O(n + m) time on an adjacency-
           216   list representation. Although computing all-pairs shortest paths (APSP) would require O(n(n + m))
           217   time, SP4LP does not perform APSP. In our experiments, we compute shortest paths only for the
           218   supervised train/validation/test links, which requires at most a small number of BFS traversals. Let
           219   S be the number of distinct source nodes among these links; the preprocessing cost is therefore
           220   O(S (n˙ + m)), typically several orders of magnitude smaller than O(n(n + m)). The resulting paths
           221   are cached and reused throughout training and inference, so no graph traversal is performed during
           222   model training or batched evaluation. In fully-inductive settings where new nodes may appear, one
           223   can compute shortest-path trees by running a single BFS per new node, providing distances and
           224   parent pointers to all existing nodes at once. This preserves the same per-link inference efficiency and
           225   avoids BFS operations per queried link. A more detailed analysis of the overall complexity, including
           226   a comparison with prior approaches, is provided in Appendix E. In practice, all shortest paths are
           227   precomputed before training, and SP4LP simply retrieves the stored sequences during training and
           228   inference. No BFS operations are performed online.
           229   SP4LP is a general and flexible framework: both the underlying GNN used to compute node
           230   representations and the sequence model used to process the embeddings along the shortest path
           231   can be chosen modularly. For instance, the GNN component can be instantiated with architectures
           232   such as GCN [18], GAT [34] or GraphSAGE [14], while the sequence model can range from simple
           233   aggregation functions like injective summation as the one proposed in Xu et al. [39], to more complex
           234   architectures such as LSTMs [15], GRU [8] or Transformers [33]. An overview of SP4LP is

           254   4.1   Further comparison with NCN

           255   NCN represents a link by aggregating the GNN embeddings of its endpoints and their common
           256   neighbors, making it particularly vulnerable to graph incompleteness. When two endpoints share no
           257   neighbors, NCN reduces to a pure GNN and can no longer leverage structural information, a far from
           258   rare situation in practice: the fraction of positive test links without common neighbors reaches 46%,
           259   55%, and 67% on Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed, and 10%, 37%, and 52% on ogbl-ppa, ogbl-citation2,
           260   and ogbl-collab (only ogbl-ddi is unaffected, at 0.04%). In contrast, SP4LP encodes the sequence of
           261   node embeddings along the shortest path between endpoints, which is always defined in connected
           262   graphs and captures richer structural patterns even in sparse or incomplete settings. Moreover, while
           263   NCN pools neighbors as unordered sets, SP4LP encodes paths as ordered sequences via LSTMs or
           264   Transformers, yielding strictly higher expressiveness (Theorem 4.5).

           265   5    Experiments

           266   We evaluate SP4LP on real-world link prediction benchmarks, where observed edges are treated as
           267   positive examples and non-edges, sampled via negative sampling, as negatives. We consider three
           268   Planetoid citation networks, Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed [40], and four OGB datasets, ogbl-citation2,
           269   ogbl-ppa, ogbl-collab and ogbl-ddi [16]. For the Planetoid datasets, we use a single fixed split across
           270   all experiments. Dataset statistics are reported in Table 6 of appendix C.
           271   As baseline methods we consider Pure GNN methods: Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) [18],
           272   Graph Attention Network (GAT) [35], GraphSAGE [14], and Graph Autoencoder (GAE) [19],
           273   and Structural Features GNN methods: SEAL [44], BUDDY [4], Neo-GNN [43], NBFNet [48],
           274   NCN [38], NCNC [38] and PEG [36], GDGNN [20], MPLP [12]. We additionally evaluate two recent
           275   link prediction models that fall outside the message-passing taxonomy of Section 3: LPFormer [29],
           276   a transformer-based model with attention over node-pair contexts, and GraphGPT [46], a generative
           277   graph transformer that linearizes graphs via Eulerian paths.
           278   As described in Section 4, SP4LP is a general framework that supports different choices of GNN
           279   backbone and sequence model ϕ (Equation 7), both treated as hyperparameters and tuned on the
           280   validation set. We explore GCN, GAT, and GraphSAGE as backbones, and LSTM, Transformer,
           281   and an injective sum aggregator [39] as sequence models. We use an MLP for ρ and, for efficiency,
           282   select the first shortest path returned by BFS as AGG. Appendix D reports implementation details,
           283   shortest-path computation, and hyperparameter configurations.

           284   Evaluation Setting We evaluate model performance under the more challenging and realistic
           285   HeaRT evaluation setting [21]. In this setting, each positive target link (i.e., an existing link) is
           286   ranked against a carefully selected set of hard negative samples (i.e., non-existing links), providing
           287   a more realistic assessment of link prediction performance in practical scenarios. We adopt two
           288   standard ranking metrics: Hits@K and Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR). Following the HeaRT protocol,
           289   we report Hits@10 and MRR for Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed, and Hits@20 along with MRR for
           290   ogbl-collab, ogbl-ddi, ogbl-ppa and ogbl-citation2. The same set of negative samples is used across all
           291   positive links, as specified in the HeaRT benchmark. The HeaRT evaluation introduces significantly
           292   harder negative samples compared to traditional evaluation settings, resulting in a more challenging
           293   and realistic benchmark. Li et al. [21] show that this leads to a substantial performance drop across

           381   6     Conclusion

           382   We introduced SP4LP, a message-passing framework for link representation that enhances the
           383   expressiveness of standard GNNs by incorporating sequential modeling over the shortest path
           384   between target nodes. Following the GNN-then-SF paradigm, SP4LP combines the efficiency of
           385   computing node embeddings only once with high expressive power, explicitly modeling multi-hop
           386   relational patterns via a sequence encoder applied to node embeddings along the shortest path. We
           387   formally proved that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than several state-of-the-art link representation
           388   models, and extensive experiments under the HeaRT evaluation protocol confirm that it achieves
           389   state-of-the-art performance across diverse datasets.
           390   Limitations. SP4LP presents two main limitations. First, it requires a preprocessing step to compute
           391   and cache shortest paths, which adds complexity compared to fully end-to-end models, although
           392   this cost is amortized across training and inference. Second, SP4LP’s advantage is reduced on
           393   graphs with very short average shortest-path length (e.g., ogbl-ddi, with average length ≈ 2), where
           394   path-based modeling has limited room to exploit structural information beyond what local methods
           395   already capture.
           396   Future work. We plan to extend SP4LP in several directions: incorporating multiple (or all) shortest
           397   paths to improve robustness and capture richer structural signals; adapting SP4LP to heterogeneous
           398   graphs, where shortest paths remain well-defined and the path encoder can naturally incorporate node
           399   and edge type embeddings; and investigating approximate shortest-path extraction techniques (e.g.,
           400   truncated BFS, landmark-based methods) to reduce preprocessing cost on very large graphs.

           536   Theorem 4.5 SP4LP is strictly more expressive than Pure GNN, NCN, BUDDY, NBFnet, GDGNN
           537   and Neo-GNN.

           538   Proof. We proceed by prove each comparison separately.

           539          • SP4LP is strictly more expressive than Pure GNN.
           540             We prove this by noting that SP4LP architecture (Equation 7) generalizes that of pure
           541             GNNs, meaning that SP4LP can simulate any pure GNN by simply ignoring the shortest
           542             path information. Thus, for any pair of non-automorphic links that a specific pure GNN can
           543             distinguish, there exists a configuration of SP4LP that distinguishes them as well. We can
           544             conclude that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than pure GNNs considering as example of
           545             pair of links indistinguishable by GNNs but distinguishable by SP4LP the one provided in
           546             the proof of Proposition 4.4.
           547          • SP4LP is strictly more expressive than NCN.
           548             We prove this in two steps: (1) When two links share no common neighbors, NCN on them
           549             reduces to a pure GNN. As we have already proved that SP4LP is strictly more expressive
           550             than pure GNNs, it follows that SP4LP is also strictly more expressive than NCN in this
           551             case. (2) When the links have common neighbors, setting AGG as summation, ρ as the
           552             Hadamard product between the endpoint representations and the concatenation with the
           553             result of the aggregation, and ϕ as the identity function, SP4LP reduces exactly to NCN.
           554             Therefore, if NCN can distinguish two links under some configuration, SP4LP can as well.
           555             By definition 2.10), this implies that SP4LP is more expressive than NCN. We can conclude
           556             that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than NCN considering the example in Figure 4. The
           557             graph is regular and thus all nodes receive the same embedding from a GNN. Consider
           558             nodes u and v: N (u) ∩ N (v) = N (u) ∩ N (v ′ ) = ∅. In this case, NCN reduces to a pure
           559             GNN and is thus unable to distinguish the links (u, v) and (u′ , v ′ ). Now, let x ∈ Rd be the
           560             representation assigned to every node by a GNN. Then, the representation of the shortest
                                   ∗                                    ∗
           561             path PG   (u, v) is simply (x, x, x), while PG (u′ , v ′ ) = (x, x, x, x). Even using a simple
           562             sum as aggregation function, SP4LP successfully distinguishes between the two links.
           563          • SP4LP is strictly more expressive than Neo-GNN and BUDDY.
           564             We have already shown that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than NCN. In Theorem 2 of
           565             the NCN paper [38], it has been proved that NCN is more expressive than both Neo-GNN
           566             and BUDDY. It follows that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than Neo-GNN and BUDDY
           567             as well.

           569              (Horizontal GDGNN). We show that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than the set-
           570             based horizontal GDGNN link encoder. First, SP4LP subsumes horizontal GDGNN: choose
           571             the SP4LP path encoder ϕ to be permutation-invariant over the nodes on a shortest path
           572             (i.e., ϕ(hP ) = POOL({hx : x ∈ P })), and choose AGG to return the representation
           573             of the same deterministically selected shortest path π(u, v) used by horizontal GDGNN
           574             (e.g., the path returned by a fixed shortest-path routine with a fixed tie-breaking rule).
           575             With the same decoder, the resulting SP4LP equals the horizontal GDGNN on every link
           576             query. To prove strictness, consider two non-automorphic links (u, v) and (u′ , v ′ ) each
           577             having a unique shortest path, P = (u, a, b, v) and P ′ = (u′ , b, a, v ′ ), with embeddings
           578             satisfying hu = hu′ , hv = hv′ , and ha ̸= hb (e.g., due to distinct node features). Any
           579             set-based horizontal GDGNN aggregates over the multiset {hu , ha , hb , hv } and hence
           580             produces identical pooled features for P and P ′ ; with matching endpoints, it outputs
           581             identical link representations and cannot distinguish the two queries. In contrast, SP4LP
           582             can choose an order-sensitive sequence encoder ϕ (e.g., an RNN/Transformer) such that
           583             ϕ(hu , ha , hb , hv ) ̸= ϕ(hu′ , hb , ha , hv′ ), and since each query has a unique shortest path,
           584             AGG is trivial. Thus SP4LP distinguishes a pair of non-automorphic link queries that
           585             horizontal GDGNN cannot, proving strictness.

           586              (Vertical GDGNN). We show that SP4LP is strictly more expressive than the vertical
           587             GDGNN link encoder. For subsumption, define the SP4LP path encoder ϕ to extract only
           588             the first and last internal nodes of a shortest path: for P = (u, x1 , . . . , xℓ−1 , v) let ϕ(hP ) =
           589             [hx1 ; hxℓ−1 ], and let AGG pool these two positions across all shortest paths (with the same
           590             permutation-invariant POOL as vertical GDGNN), yielding exactly the pooled feature over
           591             the vertical endpoint-neighbor set S V (u, v) = (N (u) ∩ W (u, v)) ∪ (N (v) ∩ W (u, v)),
           592             where W (u, v) = {w : d(u, w) + d(w, v) = d(u, v)}. Using the same decoder ρ,
           593             SP4LP matches the degree-free vertical GDGNN on every link query. For strictness,
           594             consider two non-automorphic link queries (u, v) and (u′ , v ′ ) with unique shortest paths
           595             P = (u, a, x, b, v) and P ′ = (u′ , a, y, b, v ′ ), where the first/last internal nodes coincide (a
           596             and b) but the middle internal node differs (x ̸= y), and assume hu = hu′ , hv = hv′ , and
           597             hx ̸= hy (e.g., via distinct features). Since each query has a unique shortest path, the degree-
           598             free vertical GDGNN uses the same vertical set S V (u, v) = {a, b} = S V (u′ , v ′ ), hence
           599             produces identical pooled features and cannot distinguish the two queries. In contrast, SP4LP
           600             may use an order-sensitive ϕ on the full path sequence, so that ϕ(hu , ha , hx , hb , hv ) ̸=
           601             ϕ(hu′ , ha , hy , hb , hv′ ) whenever hx ̸= hy , and AGG is trivial under uniqueness of the
           602             shortest path. Therefore, SP4LP distinguishes a pair of non-automorphic link queries that
           603             the degree-free vertical GDGNN cannot, proving strictness.

           604             Remark (Scope). The strictness result applies to the degree-free vertical GDGNN class.
           605             If the vertical model is augmented with additional induced-subgraph structural features (e.g.,
           606             degree-in-induced-subgraph), the inclusion and strictness relationships may change and
           607             require a separate analysis.

           688   E    Computational Complexity
           689   In this section, we analyze the computational complexity of our proposed SP4LP model for link
           690   prediction, following the formalism and notation introduced in [38]. This framework allows a direct
           691   comparison with prior approaches such as GAE, Neo-GNN, BUDDY, PEG, SEAL, NCN, and NCNC.
           692   Let n be the number of nodes, m the number of edges, ∆ the maximum degree, d the dimensionality
           693   of node features or embeddings, t the number of target links to predict, and k the length of the shortest
           694   path between node pairs (typically k ≪ n). We express total time complexity as O(B + C · t), where
           695   B is the precomputation cost (independent of t), and C is the per-link cost.
           696   Our SP4LP method follows the GNN-then-Structural-Feature (SF) paradigm [38], applying a
           697   Message Passing Neural Network once across the entire graph to compute node embeddings, then
           698   leveraging shortest path extraction combined with sequence modeling for each candidate link.
           699   Following the paradigm, the precomputation cost of SP4LP is:
                                                          B = O(n∆d + nd2 + Tsp )
           700   where: n∆d accounts for neighborhood aggregation in the GNN, nd2 represents linear transforma-
           701   tions in GNN layers, Tsp is the cost of computing shortest paths (e.g., via BFS).
           702   For sparse graphs, single-source BFS runs in O(m) time. Although all-pairs shortest paths (APSP)
           703   would cost O(nm), SP4LP never computes APSP. In all benchmark settings, we compute shortest
           704   paths only for the supervised train/validation/test links. Let S be the number of distinct endpoint
           705   nodes among these links. The total preprocessing cost is therefore
                                                                Tsp = O(S · m),
           706   typically several orders of magnitude smaller than O(nm). Once these paths are cached, no BFS
           707   is performed during training or batched inference. In fully inductive scenarios, shortest-path trees
           708   for new nodes can be obtained via a single BFS per new node, which provides distances and parent
           709   pointers to all existing nodes at once. The per-link inference cost of SP4LP is thus
                                                               C = O(kd + Tseq ),
           710   where k is the path length and d the embedding dimension, making it independent of the graph size n.
           711   Tseq denotes the cost of a sequence model (e.g., LSTM, Transformer) applied to the node embeddings
           712   along that path.
           713   Importantly, since both the GNN embeddings and shortest paths can be precomputed, B is incurred
           714   only once. Given that k is typically small in practice, C remains low even at scale. Unlike subgraph-
           715   based methods such as SEAL, which require per-link GNN inference over extracted neighborhoods,
           716   SP4LP performs lightweight sequence modeling on compact paths, supporting efficient batched
           717   inference. Additionally, the framework is flexible: Tsp can be cached or approximated [30], and Tseq
           718   depends on the chosen architecture and path length.
           719   We summarize the complexities in the following table:
           720   All methods conform to the form O(B + C · t), with a one-time graph-level computation and a
           721   per-target-link cost. In the table, h is the cost of the hash function used in BUDDY, l denotes the radius
           722   of local neighborhoods in Neo-GNN and SEAL, and D denotes the number of Laplacian eigenvectors
           723   used for spectral positional encoding in PEG. GAE, NCN, and BUDDY are efficient but limited in
           724   expressiveness. NCNC enhances NCN via soft neighbor completion at a slightly higher cost. PEG
           725   incorporates spectral encoding (O(nD2 )), while SEAL is the most computationally intensive due
           726   to subgraph extraction and per-link GNN processing. SP4LP, in contrast, strikes a balance between
           727   efficiency and expressiveness by combining GNN-based embeddings with sequence modeling over
           728   shortest paths, whose cost can be mitigated via approximation techniques [30], ensuring scalability
           729   for large graphs.
